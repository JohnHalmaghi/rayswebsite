    • About Me

    Twenty-five years ago, I was born in Clovis, CA and lived there until I left for college. When I was ten, I decided that I was going to grow up to become a teacher because of the influence that my teachers had on me. I also knew I would attend San Diego State University, and boy was my ten-year-old self certain of these two decisions. My high-school self… not so much.     Teaching Philosophy

    Throughout my education, I have shifted my understanding of what it means to be an effective teacher. At first, I wanted to be a teacher who would present creative lessons each week, primarily because I thought that fun lessons in and of themselves would keep the students engaged and entertained, and partly because I thought it would make me a better teacher in the eyes of my students and peers. However, during my student teaching, I quickly learned that lesson plans do not include classroom and time management skills and not everything will go as perfectly as I envisioned. My students and peers will not necessarily think I am the best teacher for how clever and innovative the lessons are, because it is a collection of skills great teachers possess that make lessons work. Students are all unique and have their own challenges and joys which a great teacher will learn and adjust to accordingly. My current philosophy on teaching has included this necessary element. I believe that students have unique strengths and do not all learn in the same ways, so they should be taught and assessed through a variety of means. This is more easily accomplished when teachers work together and with their learning community.

    How I have learned about my students’ strengths and interests was through personality questions given at the beginning of the year. I asked any question I felt would help me understand how my students think and, hopefully, how they enjoy learning. While this questionnaire helped, I usually learned the most about my students throughout the school year. For example, during my final semester of student teaching I started to see that every time I or my master teacher used a video in a lesson, the students would all love it. Even students who often were inattentive in class were instead fixed on the video and remembered it well. As a result, I tried to include short, relevant videos in many of my lessons, whereas in another class I may not have chosen to do that.

    As for assessment of students, I believe differentiated assessment is crucial to measuring learning growth in a whole class setting. Some students are excellent test-takers and are truly able to show how much they have learned through traditional exams, but many students have a tough time expressing their knowledge of a subject with this method. I intend to use project-based assessment in my future classroom because it is versatile (meaning students could choose what kind of project to create), could be done in groups, and helps in assessment of students who may not speak or read English at a fluent level. This type of assessment could be done while in the midst of learning a topic, or summatively. I also intend to measure growth of student knowledge through continual assessment.

    While I am able to identify student needs and assess their academic growth, I certainly value collaboration among colleagues to aid in these endeavors. Working as a team to present lessons based on state standards will be extremely beneficial to the entire grade level, because the teachers are helping each other be as prepared, knowledgable, and confident as possible every day. Speaking as a new educator, any aid and guidance given to me will help me be a better teacher for my students and a better employee for the school and district.

    Knowing that I will someday be part of a learning community that includes my students, fellow teachers, parents, and administration makes me hopeful and excited because I will have a role in raising up the next generation. I understand the weight of my responsibility to teach students how to be effective learners first and foremost, but also how to be good citizens, athletes, artists, role models, and friends. Each student has a unique past and, therefore, a unique future, and I will strive to bring about opportunities that enlighten them of their talents and encourage them to have a love of learning.

    Experience

    While at San Diego State University, my major was Liberal Studies with a focus in science. As required by California, I completed 45 hours of observation for my Early Field Experience in the La Mesa-Spring Valley and Santee School Districts while attending SDSU. It was beneficial to take part in reading groups, observe various types of classroom management strategies, and begin to understand how students learn best; however, most of my most valuable experience was gained through student teaching.

    I earned my preliminary multiple subject credential through Fresno State, and all of my student teaching was completed in Clovis Unified School District. Because I grew up in this district, it was easy to assimilate to the classrooms and refamiliarize myself with the district’s expectations of its students. The program was three semesters long, each semester in a different class and grade level. Semester 1 was spent mostly observing in a 4/5 combo class, but I often worked with small groups teaching reading and social studies lessons. Semester 2, I was assigned to a 1st grade class, in which I lead morning routine and taught many lessons on my own across multiple subjects. I gained experience in conducting case studies with an advanced student, an English learner, and a special needs student. My final semester, I worked in a 6th grade class, and it was incredibly immersive as I was in the classroom full-time. I took over the class for two weeks straight, with minimal assistance, and gained experience working with struggling readers and creating assessment plans. I also had the opportunity to attend their 6th grade camp.

    Since graduating, I have been subbing in various districts in the San Diego county, and observing in junior high and elementary school classrooms whenever I get the chance. It has been beneficial to be a substitute teacher as I have grown in my time management and classroom management skills, which was always a focus of mine in the credential program. I am still learning and know I will always have more to learn in this wonderful profession.
